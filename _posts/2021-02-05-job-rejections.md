<p><img alt="Image result for job rejection" class="n3VNCb" data-noaft="1" height="125" jsaction="load:XAeZkd;" jsname="HiaYvf" src="https://insights.dice.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/08/Job-rejection-email-letter-Dice.png" width="200"/></p><p>There are people, I know, who secure immediately every job they apply for. I am not one of those lucky people. I know as well as anybody the sting of disappointment that accompanies a rejection email. The sting gets worse, of course, the further along the process you get; the most painful rejections come when you've been to two or three interviews with a school, taught a lesson, done a presentation and even started to get to know some of the staff. </p><p>Right now we're in the process of recruiting for roles at <a href="https://www.malverncollege.ch" target="_blank">Malvern College Switzerland</a> and so I have found myself on the other side of the fence, having to disappoint people. If you're one of those people, I feel your pain; this little post is for you. </p><p>I've tried to list the reasons why people's applications failed to get through to the shortlist stage. This, I hope, will give a bit more insight into the process than the: <i>'It was a strong field' </i>brush off that is rather too common in job application correspondence, but that helps nobody. </p><p>You may have failed to make the shortlist because your application displayed one or more of the problems listed below. Only a tiny handful of these mistakes are immediately 'fatal' but when placed in a pool of applications without them they place your application at a disadvantage; and that, in a large field, is all it takes not to make it to the shortlist. So here we go:</p><p>Things you can fix:</p><ol><li>Not following the rubric. We said we didn't want CVs and yet you attached one. We asked you to apply by the TES and yet you emailed us direct etc.</li><li>Sloppy spelling or, more common, sloppy capitalisation. This affected more applications than, as teachers, we should be happy with. I think people have become inured to a sort of digital slang that is seen as acceptable on an online form. It leaves a bad impression.</li><li>Asking questions like: 'Will my sofa fit in the flat?' before having even been contacted by the school. Questions like this (and I put in this bracket questions about money) give the impression your primary interest isn't the role itself.</li><li>Asking questions that are answered in the applicant pack and/or on the website.</li><li>Copying and pasting a personal statement that was clearly made with another school in mind.</li><li>Writing a perfunctory personal statement of only a couple of sentences in length.</li><li>Not writing a personal statement at all and instead saying something like: 'My CV speaks for itself.'</li><li>Leaving gaps in your employment history. However long and tedious, this part of the form needs to be filled in assiduously. You're applying for a role in a school, there will be lots of children about, so there's a non-negotiable safeguarding element to this.</li><li>Not providing references at the outset. Asking us not to contact them before deferring to you is fine, but leaving that section of the form completely blank rings alarm bells.</li><li>Denigrating - even obliquely - a former employer. Maybe they were horrible and you firmly occupy the moral high ground but we haven't met you yet and so for us, it's 50/50. Those aren't great odds.</li><li>Leaving other gaps on the application form. If a section really doesn't apply write N/A. A blank leaves you open to the suspicion that you haven't taken sufficient care.</li></ol><p>Things you can't (immediately) fix, but that nevertheless, <i>all other things being equal</i>, did factor in decision making:</p><ol><li>Not offering the right level of flexibility in terms of additional subjects taught. </li><li>Not having A-Level and GCSE experience (or having less than an otherwise equivalent candidate).</li><li>Not having boarding experience (or having less than an otherwise equivalent candidate).</li><li>Not having the requisite co-curricular and extra-curricular experience, or not showcasing this sufficiently.</li><li>Having a career history that suggests you've moved around rather too often, never staying put anywhere for long.</li><li>Not having the necessary experience of KS4 and 5. We had a lot of applicants who were excellent, but had never taught post-16.</li></ol><div>Finally, as a startup, we have been very keen to 'get the right people on the bus' as quickly as possible so that we can begin planning for the tasks that lie ahead. This has meant that we have shortlisted for some positions - as we said we would in the application pack - before the application deadline. Annoying though this is, it is just possible that you were a perfect candidate but that we never got to see you because we'd already appointed by the time your application was submitted. Also, the need for some positions diminished, with the recruitment of teachers able to teach multiple disciplines.</div><p>So there you have it. Helpful, I hope.</p><p><b>****Addendum****</b></p><p>Since writing this post I have become aware of other reasons why candidates might not be accepted to international schools. To be fair, none of these applied at the time we were appointing our first wave of candidates at MCS, but I do know that they can occur at the murkier end of the international market and in schools operating in countries where such things are the norm.</p><p>In such situations, the people who interview you may not be those who make the final decision. Decisions not to appoint can be made for a whole range of reasons which you as a candidate cannot account for. They include:</p><ul><li><b>Loss of bottle </b>- The ultimate decision maker decides that the budget won't stretch to another candidate and so despite having jumped through all the hoops you're not appointed.</li><li><b>Changes of plan</b> - X was going to teach Y but it now transpires can also teach Z and so things have been reorganised to make do without your services.</li><li><b>Racism/xenophobia</b> - Never openly admitted to, of course, but some schools won't appoint certain groups because 'parents won't like it'. Awful, but it does happen.</li><li><b>Visa issues etc</b>. - You may have been the perfect candidate but because of your passport it's either impossible or too difficult to get the right visa for you to work in the school.</li><li><b>Ignorance</b> - Those making the ultimate decision may not have much experience in education. If your qualifications are unusual in any way they may see it as too much of a risk to appoint you.</li><li><b>Delusion </b>- Some decision-makers think that they're going to be able to fill their positions entirely with the 'very best' (Oxbridge educated, first-class degrees etc.) They will reject candidates who don't meet these ideals until doing so becomes completely untenable - and possibly beyond this point.</li><li><b>Cost concerns</b> - You have several children, say, and the school is already running tight margins. A cheaper child-free member of staff may be sought even if you're objectively the best candidate.</li></ul>
