<span style="text-align: left;">Today's @teachthought 30-day blogging challenge asks bloggers:</span></div>
<blockquote class="tr_bq">
<span style="font-style: italic;">Do you have other hobbies/interests that you bring into your classroom teaching? Explain.</span></blockquote>
I'm very lucky. There is a big overlap between the things I enjoy and the things I must do in my day-to-day working life:<br />
<div>
<ol>
<li><div>
I like tinkering with&nbsp;computers, finding new apps and widgets and putting these to use in the classroom. I just have to be careful that the cult of the new doesn't interfere with delivering the content I need to get across.</div>
</li>
<li><div>
I spend most of my holidays up in the&nbsp;<a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lake_District" shape="rect" target="_blank">Lake District</a>. This really is a geographers'&nbsp;paradise. I even weave in a weekend trip up to&nbsp;<a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Borrowdale" shape="rect" target="_blank">Borrowdale</a>&nbsp;for an U6th module on glacial geomorphology. Seventh heaven.</div>
</li>
<li><div>
I really enjoy reading and listening to podcasts. Current affairs interest me and so I am able to make my teaching relevant. Geographers - much maligned as pencil-sharpening rugger-buggers - actually have a lot to say about the world.</div>
</li>
<li><div>
I keep myself fit and recently have taken to setting myself big physical challenges. Last year I did&nbsp;<a href="http://www.cyclingforcharity.org/" shape="rect" target="_blank">this</a>, this year I intend to do&nbsp;<a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bob_Graham_Round" shape="rect" target="_blank">this</a>. I like to think that these things give me a bit of a spring to my stride and make my lessons more energetic than they might otherwise be.</div>
</li>
</ol>
</div>
